What Does This Project Do?

In our dataset, there is information on neighborhood, neighborhood group, room type, minimum number of days, and more that correlate with each other in intriguing ways. Our goal is to predict the price of an Airbnb listing in New York City, which will be measured in US dollars.

Why This Project?

Currently, it is very popular to place a property on the Airbnb market to make some extra cash and even have a career if one owns a very nice property. Given this trend, we were inspired to create a machine learning model that can help potential hosts and tourists alike. It can help potential hosts decide whether it would be profitable to place their property on the market given its location, room type, and more. As for the tourists, they can use our model to verify that the renting price is reasonable. Another final justification was that this was a viable data set, large enough for us to ensure reasonably accurate results, but not so large that our computers could still handle it without crashing.

Visualizations


Minimum Nights vs. Number of Reviews

One correlation we have found is that the number of reviews decreases with the increase in minimum nights. This shows that the longer the required minimum nights in an Airbnb, the fewer reviews it will recieve. Most people want to rent an Airbnb with lots of reviews to ensure the quality of the house and it is a good insight to know that a lower number of reviews is acceptable if the minimum number of nights is higher.


Scatterplot: Availability and Price

Initially, we thought that availability would have a distinct effect on the price because of seasonal demand. However, we were wrong. In this scatter plot, we can see that it does not matter how available the listing is as it does not affect the price. This graph also reaffirms that the most expensive listings are in Manhattan while the cheapest listings are in the Bronx.


Violin Plot: Borough and Price

A violin plot is used to visualize the distribution of data. The width of it relates to the frequency of data points in that region. For example, Manhattan (red) has a very narrow violin shape, indicating that there are not many listings with the price of the median ($141).


Manhattan (red) had the highest median price ($141) and its $45-141 range had the most distributed values. Brooklyn (blue) had the second highest median price ($90) and the most distributed values were in the range of $25-90, the widest of all the boroughs. Staten Island (purple) and Queens (green) had the same median price ($75) and same range for the most distributed values ($48-75) but differed slightly in its maximum prices. Lastly, the Bronx (orange) had the lowest median price ($65) and most of its distributed values were in the range of $40-65.


Heatmap of All Variables in the Dataset

For the most part, there were very negative correlations between the variables as seen by price and longitude and somewhat negative correlations between the variables as seen by availability and calculated host listings. There was a slightly positive correlation between reviews per month and number of reviews.


Box Plot of New York Boroughs and Price

This graph shows the minimum, maximum, and median prices of a listing in each of the boroughs. Overall, all of these box plots are top skewed. The median is typically between 65 and 141 for the different boroughs. The medians further confirm that Manhattan is typically the most expensive borough as opposed to the Bronx being the cheapest borough. There are also apparent outliers for all of the boroughs that would matter for the machine learning models.


Bar Chart of Room Type and Median Price

The highest median listing price is the "Entire home/apt" at $160. The second highest median listing price is "Single" private rooms at $70. The lowest median listing price is the "Shared Room" at $45. This shows that when renting an entire home/apt, you will be paying more than you would be for the single or shared rooms

Machine Learning


Linear Regression: Results and Explanation

First, we tried a baseline linear regression model. This model basically uses a line to predict the price of the Airbnb. Linear regression assumes that all features have a linear relation with price of an Airbnb in New York City, so we can fit a line of best fit to predict future values. After testing the model with our dataset, we got a root mean square error of 74 which isn't great as most of our values got to around 40 and the higher the RMSE is, the worse it is. However, it's still not terrible.

K Nearest Neighbors: Results and Explanation

The next model we used was K Nearest Neighbors. The KNN machine learning algorithm looks at data points in the testing set and predicts a price based on its similarity to other data points in the testing set. The model creates groups of similar data points and matches the input variables to one of those groups. Our model created thousands of groups out of our 43,000 data points and has an RMSE value of 43.212.

Random Forest Regression: Results and Explanation

The Random Forest Regression model uses multiple decision trees with random decisions to create outputs and trains itself on which ones work. The RMSE we got from the model was 43.8 which is pretty good but not the best we got from any of the models.

LightGBM: Results and Explanation

The Light Gradient Boosting Machine model also known as LightGBM, is very similar to the random forest regression model except it uses the outputs of its trees for inputs of other trees. Its RMSE was 44 which isn't terrible, but we had better RMSEs from other models.

Stacking: Results and Explanation

Stacking is a model in which a single model is used to learn how to combine predictions from contributing models to create a linear regression. We chose this model because the RMSE value of 41.595 was lower than others, and therefore more accurate.

3D Plot of Principal Components

PCA is used to combat the issues of high-dimensional data by way of dimensionality reduction. We reduced the number of colums we had while keeping 98.22% of the varience by using Principal Component Analysis. Essentially PCA helps to find patterns in the high-dimensional datasets. We made a 3D graph to show the reduced 3 columns in 3 principle components. We would be able to use the new data in a new model to see if the accuracy increased or decreased.


Conclusion


In conclusion, Stacking performed the best on our dataset for predicting the price of an Airbnb in New York. Positively correlated features with price like number of reviews per month and neighborhoods closer to Manhattan along with negatively correlated features like minimum number of days helped us achieve these results.

So what?

So what is the significance of our project? Because we know which features are most correlated with price, we can identify both the factors that lead to a higher priced Airbnb and also predict what it would cost to stay in an Airbnb in New York.
